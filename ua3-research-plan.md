## Target customer

- **Current claim:** An engaged citizen who cares about conservation and wants to learn more about birds and wants all the data and info in one app.
- **Questions:** Where do conservation-minded birdwatchers gather online?
- **Sources:** Facebook groups, Reddit, birding forums, conservation org communities
- **Enough:** Find at least 10 distinct groups or communities where this audience gathers.
- **Expected findings:** Facebook groups are the primary gathering place; thousands of people in this category.
- **Log:**
  - 2026-07-01, "conservation minded birdwatchers online communities Facebook Reddit forums" → BirdForum is the internet's largest birding community; Audubon Society has 500+ local chapters in North America; eBird is the world's largest bird citizen science program with tens of thousands of contributors; Global Big Day drew 14,000 volunteers from 135 countries. Facebook groups exist at regional/state level for birding and Audubon chapters. **Interpretation:** Audience is larger than predicted (hundreds of thousands, not thousands) and gathers across multiple platforms, not just Facebook. "Engaged citizen" still fits as a description — the research confirms this is a real, reachable group. Stopping criterion met (found well over 10 distinct communities).

## Need or opportunity

- **Current claim:** Wants to have data points of each species of bird in a location in one place to take action and advocate for the birds in the habitat and spread awareness.
- **Questions:** Are people actively frustrated about not having bird population data in one place?
- **Sources:** Reddit (r/birdwatching, r/ornithology), app store reviews, birding forums
- **Enough:** 10 sources or discussions showing the need exists.
- **Expected findings:** Latent need — people would recognize it if shown the solution, but not actively complaining.
- **Log:**
  - 2026-07-01, "birdwatching app fragmented tools switching apps citizen science data access problem" → eBird's lack of integrated species guide called a "major drawback"; switching apps described as something "nobody likes doing." Birda integrating ID+logging but not population trends. No direct evidence of frustration about missing trend data for advocacy specifically. **Interpretation:** Fragmentation pain is real and active; advocacy use case is not yet confirmed by research — needs interviews. Clause revised to add "in one place" to reflect the fragmentation finding.

## Primary competitive alternative

- **Current claim:** Google, eBird, and other tools not designed for non-experts.
- **Questions:** What apps already exist for bird population trends? Do any serve non-expert conservation advocates?
- **Sources:** App stores, birding websites, conservation org sites
- **Enough:** 3 apps found and evaluated.
- **Expected findings:** Something similar exists for population trends, but not in an accessible all-in-one format.
- **Log:**
  - 2026-07-01, "apps bird population trends over time conservation data visualization citizen science" → Found eBird (Cornell, largest platform, has Status & Trends maps but built for serious birders), NatureCounts (Birds Canada, newly launched, tracks population trends), BirdCast (migration forecasting), Bird Conservation Network rolling out Tableau visualization in 2026. **Interpretation:** Real tools exist but are built for researchers and expert birders, not for engaged citizens seeking accessible advocacy tools. Competitive alternative clause updated from "Google and scattered online sources" to "Google, eBird, and other tools not designed for non-experts." Stopping criterion met (3+ apps found).

## Primary differentiation

- **Current claim:** Allows users to see trends of bird data points all in one place and accessible to non-experts.
- **Questions:** Are eBird and similar tools genuinely hard for non-experts to use for trend data?
- **Sources:** eBird documentation, app store reviews, Reddit
- **Enough:** 5 sources confirming the accessibility gap.
- **Expected findings:** eBird is genuinely hard for non-experts, not just slightly less convenient.
- **Log:**
  - 2026-07-01, eBird documentation review → Advanced trend features require RStudio scripts, statistical modeling, and step-by-step coding tutorials. Population trend tools described in terms of "scientists and practitioners." **Interpretation:** eBird's trend data tools assume technical expertise — confirmed that the accessibility gap is real, even if no direct user complaints were found. Differentiation clause updated to add "accessible to non-experts." Partial confirmation of prediction (3 indirect sources, not 5 direct — advocacy use case still needs interview confirmation).
